\section{Part 3: Warehouse Operations Solutions}\label{part3}

\subsection{Question 3.1: Slotting Optimization}\label{q3.1}

Using the ABC-XYZ classification from Part 1 and the physical SKU attributes from the
Master Data, we assigned all 470 classified SKUs to three warehouse zones.
We present two models: \textbf{Model 1} (ABC-only baseline) and
\textbf{Model 2} (ABC + Ergonomics + Pick-Mix), which achieves the
\textbf{30\% peak-time improvement target} through ergonomic sub-tiering.

\subsubsection{Model 1 --- ABC-Only Baseline}\label{model1}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pick-Face Zone (Ground Level)} --- Class A SKUs: lowest racks at
          ergonomic height, nearest to the I/O dock and packing station.
    \item \textbf{Forward Reserve Zone} --- Class B SKUs: two-bin replenishment logic.
          A forward pick-face bin is replenished from the bulk reserve (WMS-triggered).
    \item \textbf{Reserve / Bulk Zone} --- Class C SKUs: upper racks and pallet block-stack.
\end{itemize}

and drive \textbf{25.88\%} of pick frequency and \textbf{57.87\%} of volume.

\paragraph{Composite intra-zone score.}
Within each sub-tier, slots are ranked by a multi-criteria composite score (adapted from Tompkins et al., 2010):
\[
score = 0.50 \times v_{rank} + 0.30 \times ergo_{penalty} + 0.20 \times size_{score}
\]
where $v_{rank}$ is the normalised velocity rank (0=fastest), $ergo_{penalty}$ is the handling weight penalty (0 / 0.5 / 1.0 for unit weight $\leq$3\,kg, 3--8\,kg, and $>$8\,kg, based on ISO 11228-1 and NIOSH manual handling guidelines), and $size_{score}$ is the CBM relative to the median of the class.

\paragraph{Pick-Time Benchmarks.}
The model uses standard pick-times (6.0\,sec/piece for loose picking from shelves, 12.0\,sec/carton for manual hand-carry, and 120.0\,sec/pallet for forklift retrieval), verified against industrial engineering MOST/MTM standards and Vietnam logistics benchmarks (1--3 minutes for forklift pallet moves; Mecalux, 2024).

\paragraph{A-Door Rule.}
The top-10 velocity SKUs are pinned to the first 10 bays adjacent to the outbound dock (dock-adjacent slotting; Frazelle, 2002), neutralizing travel time for peak throughput bursts.

\subsubsection{Quantitative Analysis: Travel-Time Proof}\label{travel-time}

\paragraph{Physical-distance model.}
Walk speed: \textbf{72 m/min} (1.2 m/s, standard warehouse); round-trip factor: \textbf{2}.
Zone distances to packing station: Zone A = 8 m (golden pick-face), Zone B = 25 m (mid-bay), Zone C = 47.5 m (reserve).
Formula:
$$\text{Total round-trip distance} = \sum_{\text{zone}} \text{picks}_{\text{zone}} \times D_{\text{zone}} \times 2$$
$$\text{Travel time (min)} = \frac{\text{Total round-trip distance}}{\text{walk speed}}$$
Baseline (random placement): every pick travels the SKU-count-weighted average zone distance:
$$\overline{D}_{\text{random}} = \frac{N_A \cdot D_A + N_B \cdot D_B + N_C \cdot D_C}{N}$$

The ABC velocity-zoned layout achieves a \textbf{35.4\% reduction} in total picker travel distance
(vs. random placement baseline avg. \textbf{43.3\,m/pick} $\rightarrow$ optimized \textbf{27.9\,m/pick}),
well above the $\geq30\%$ target.
This comes from concentrating the highest-frequency Class~A SKUs (5{,}767 picks) at 8\,m,
versus placing them randomly at the warehouse average 43.3\,m.
\textbf{Model 2 (ABC + Ergonomics)} adds sub-tier rules (A1 pallet lane, A2 big-face, B1 bulk-replen)
that further reduce MHE conflicts and ergonomic penalties, yielding an additional estimated
15--40\% pick-throughput improvement on top of the distance savings (industry benchmark: Dematic, MHI).
This additional throughput gain comes from three mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Forklift separation} (A1 sub-tier): pallet-dominant SKUs are slotted in a
        dedicated ground-level forklift lane, preventing MHE from blocking carton pickers
        during peak waves --- estimated +8--10\% throughput.
  \item \textbf{Big-face ergonomics} (A2 sub-tier): carton-dominant A-X SKUs at waist height
        eliminate bending/overhead reach; at 800+ picks/day peak this saves $\approx$3--5\,sec/pick.
  \item \textbf{A-door pinning}: top-10 velocity SKUs within 5 rack bays of the dock
        reduce travel by $\approx$5--8\% on peak-hour bursts.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Layout Recommendations}\label{layout}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{U-flow configuration}: Receiving and shipping docks on the same wall;
          highest-convenience slots cluster at the dock end.
    \item \textbf{A1 Pallet Lane}: dedicated forklift aisle along the dock wall for
          pallet-dominant AZ/AY SKUs --- separates MHE from manual pickers.
    \item \textbf{A2 Big-Face Shelf}: AX carton-dominant SKUs at waist-to-shoulder height
          (0.8--1.6\,m), full-carton facing, eliminates bending and overhead reach.
    \item \textbf{B1 Bulk-Replen Lane}: B-class pallet/large-CBM items stored at
          mid-aisle ground level with a dedicated replenishment travel path.
    \item \textbf{Two-bin for B2}: Forward pick-face bin replenished from bulk
          reserve when on-hand drops below ROP (WMS-triggered).
    \item \textbf{C1 Upper Rack / C2 Floor Block}: slow-movers segregated by size;
          small SKUs overhead, large SKUs block-stacked at floor level behind Zone B.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Question 3.2: Omni-Channel Pick-and-Pack Process Design}\label{q3.2}

The outbound fulfillment process uses distinct operational pathways for B2B and B2C
channels, unified by a structured inventory allocation policy when stock is contested.

\subsubsection{B2B Pathway — Pallet / Total Picking}\label{b2b-path}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Order release}: WMS validates on-hand stock against the B2B
          purchase order (contractual quantities and lead times).
    \item \textbf{Pick instruction}: Full-pallet or full-CTN pick task issued to operator.
    \item \textbf{Pick execution}: Operator uses forklift or reach truck to retrieve
          entire pallet(s) from the Forward Reserve Zone (B) or Reserve/Bulk Zone (C).
          Items bypass individual sortation.
    \item \textbf{B2B QC Station}: Wrap inspection, quantity verification, and
          outbound labeling.
    \item \textbf{Dispatch}: Pallets staged at outbound dock, loaded onto FTL truck.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{B2C / E-Commerce Pathway — Batch Picking + Zone Routing}\label{b2c-path}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Batch wave release}: Multiple B2C orders are grouped into a single
          pick wave based on SKU proximity and delivery-window urgency.
    \item \textbf{Batch pick-to-cart}: Picker traverses Zone 1 (Pick-Face Zone) in a
          single pass with a multi-compartment cart, collecting all items for the wave.
    \item \textbf{Sort \& consolidate}: At the sortation station, items are separated
          by individual order and consolidated into shipment boxes.
    \item \textbf{QC + pack}: Bubble wrap or polybag packing, shipping label applied.
    \item \textbf{Dispatch}: Parcels handed to last-mile courier.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Stock Allocation Conflict and Escalation Policy}\label{conflict}

When the same SKU is ordered simultaneously by a B2B client and a B2C customer
and on-hand stock is insufficient for both, the system resolves the shortage in two stages:

\paragraph{Stage 1 — Automated Allocation (WMS).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Rule 1 (SLA Protection)}: Allocate the full contractual quantity to
          the B2B order first, preventing penalty fees for under-delivery.
    \item \textit{Rule 2 (Pro-rata B2C)}: Distribute remaining stock to B2C orders
          pro-rata, with priority to orders with the shortest delivery windows.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Stage 2 — Managerial Escalation (if Stage 1 insufficient).}
The WMS raises a critical ticket to the \textbf{Inventory Control \& Demand Planning Manager},
who can execute one or more of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Action 1 (Safety Stock Draw)}: Release uncommitted buffer safety stock
          from the common pool (pre-computed for all Class A SKUs in Q2.2).
    \item \textit{Action 2 (Emergency Transfer)}: Initiate an inter-hub transfer
          between My Phuoc and Vinh Loc warehouses, leveraging their proximity
          within the Greater Ho Chi Minh City area.
    \item \textit{Action 3 (Partial Shipment)}: Contact the B2B client to obtain approval
          for a partial fulfillment; B2C customers notified of delay with revised ETA.
\end{itemize}

Upon resolution, ERP and WMS records are updated and adjusted pick lists are
released to the warehouse floor. Figure \ref{fig:q32-flowchart} illustrates the
